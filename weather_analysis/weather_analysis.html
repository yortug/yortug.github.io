Water Scarcity¶

Given the exploration of the data here I don't think we should be too concerned with water scarcity within Perth. I managed to grab some historical data for the average monthly rainfall in Perth (https://www.watercorporation.com.au/water-supply/rainfall-and-dams/rainfall), and when plotting it ontop of this 2016 & 2017 data set I can't see any significant differences; that is, differences that would prompt heavy water regulation.

However, it is clear that during the 2016 Winter we definitely didn't get as much rain as we should have expected but I would just put that down to the general variance within the weather. The dry 2016 Winter was also somewhat redeemed by quite a wet 2016/17 Summer/Spring; specifically on the 10th of February. The average total monthly rainfall for February is usually 12.5mm; for the whole month. On the 10th of February alone we measured 114mm of rainfall, that's nearly 10x the expected monthly rainfall! Another example of what I believe to just be variance at play.

Too get a more accurate estimate on how concerned we should be about water scarcity we would need a larger data set with more variables. Things like population growth, average amount of water used per person, the levels of our major dams in WA over time, how much these dams increase due to rainfall, how much they decrease due to evaporation, etc, etc.

But just going of the basis of this dataset and historical mean monthly rainfall averages I'd say water scarcity isn't too big of a deal.

Wind Power¶

According to http://www.culturechange.org/wind.htm annual average wind speeds of 9mph are required for a small wind turbine, where as a utility scale wind power turbine might require 13mph. I'll pick in the middle of these two values and assume that in general 11mph (roughly 18km/h) winds would be enough to power your average wind turbine.

I think that given the exploration I've done with the different wind speeds and the time of the maximum wind speed occurance, wind power would be quite suitable for the Perth region. Although the majority of our 9am wind speeds are below 18km/h, there is still a decent chunk which are making in above that 18km/h mark. When we look at the 3pm wind speeds we can see that they are more consistenly around that 18km/h mark; the trend is quite similar to the 9am wind speeds however it's just a tiny bit faster on average. And then there are the maximum wind speeds which only dipe below 18km/h on a handful of occassions; indicating that nearly every single day we have at least some wind which will be able to spin a wind turbing.

Considering we only have 9am, 3pm and maximum wind speed data I was curious as to when these maximum speeds occur. It may be that 9am and 3pm are some of the slower parts of the day for wind speed; wind could pick up later in the evening, the middle of the night, or even in the early hours of the morning and we wouldn't be taking that account in our data. From the data I looked at it showed the median of the max wind gust occuring between 2pm - 3pm, and a most often occuring between 4pm - 5pm; none of these time slots are shown within our data set. This tells me that after the 9am wind speeds are taken, the wind could start the pick up around midday, then start to die off around when when the 3pm wind speeds are taken, and then start to pick up once again.

This gives even more fuel to wind power being suitable for the Perth region because our 9am wind speeds are pretty close to turning the turbine and our 3pm wind speeds are turning it close to half the time. So if the wind in fact did start to pick up after these time slots we'd be spinning the turbine most ofthe day, and even a lot of the night!

Solar Power¶

According to https://www.solarchoice.net.au/residential/residential-solar-power the average Australian family would need a 2.25kWh solar system to cover most of their electrical needs completely. They estimate that this system would produce somewhere between 9kWh - 13kWh per day for the average Perth household (https://www.solarchoice.net.au/blog/wp-content/uploads/Average-Daily-Production-of-Renewable-Energy.jpg). This lines up with https://www.billrepublic.com/average-electricity-usage/ who estimate that per year the average Australian household in 2014 used 5817kWh of electricity; which turns out to equate to roughly 16kWh of energy used per day - not too far off the estimate for the average Perth household. I'm going to compare what these two websites are claiming against the data collected from the BOM, and some rough assumptions made around how cloud cover affects solar pannel efficiency.

According to https://www.solarpowerauthority.com/how-to-calculate-your-peak-sun-hours/ clouds covering the Sun reduce a solar pannel's efficiency by about 25% - 40%; meaning that they're producing about 60% - 75% of their normal electricity output during this cloudy weather.

Let's make some more assumptions; I'm going to say that during winter the Sun will be shining on our solar pannel for a possible 7 hours, and in summer it'll be more like 9 hours. Looking at the sunshine over time plot below we can see that during our least sunny days (autumn, winter/start of spring) we average around 5 hours of sunlight. However, during the more sunny days (end of spring, summer) we are almost consistently getting over 10 hours of direct sunlight.

Roughly looking at both of our cloud cover plots we can see that in about 65% of these least sunny days the majority of the sky is covered in clouds; the other 35% of these days we have roughly no clouds or around a quarter of the sky covered in clouds (still a decent amount for the Sun to hit our solar pannel). Looking at the more sunny days we can see that the proportions are sort of switched. On about 75% of these more sunny days there is roughly no cloud cover or one eighth of the sky covered (barely anything). And then on the other 25% of the sunny days it's still only about half of the sky covered in clouds; rarely is the whole sky covered in clouds during summer.

From all these assumptions I'll create the following equations:

So by making some assumptions of solar pannel efficiency, and by looking at our plots to estimate how much cloud cover and sunlight we might expect on any given winter or summer day; we have calculated that our solar pannel would produce 5590kWh of electricity every year. This is 96% of the (2014) total average electricity usage for an Australian household. Meaning that, by these calculations, your average Perth resident would on only be paying for 4% of their total years worth of electricity bills if they have a solar pannel system installed.

This alone shows that solar power is perfectly suitable for a Perth household! And with solar cells becoming even more efficient at absorbing sunlight, and with the technology to store this energy constantly advancing - you'd be spending money on electricity you could be saving if you don't install solar pannels!
